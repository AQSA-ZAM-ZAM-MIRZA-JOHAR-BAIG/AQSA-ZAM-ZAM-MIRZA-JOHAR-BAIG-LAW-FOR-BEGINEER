## Introduction

A **contract** is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties. But not every promise is a contract. If you promise your friend you will buy them lunch and then you don't, they cannot sue you for breach of contract. 

So, what turns a simple promise into a legal contract?

## The 3 Essential Elements

For a contract to be legally valid and enforceable in a court of law, it typically needs three essential ingredients:

1.  **Offer and Acceptance:** One party must make a clear offer (*"I will sell you my car for $5,000"*), and the other party must mirror that offer with an acceptance (*"I accept your offer to buy your car for $5,000"*).
2.  **Consideration:** This is the "price" paid for the promise. Both parties must exchange something of value. Value doesn't just mean money; it can be goods, services, or a promise *not* to do something.
3.  **Intention to Create Legal Relations:** Both parties must understand and intend that their agreement will be legally binding. Promises between family members (like buying your kid an ice cream) generally lack this intention.

*Note: The parties must also have the legal capacity to contract (e.g., they must be of sound mind and legal adult age).*

## Forms of Contracts

Contracts **do not have to be in writing** to be legally binding. A verbal agreement can be a contract! However, verbal contracts are extremely difficult to prove in court. That is why having important agreements written down and signed is always the best practice.

## Real-Life Example

> **Scenario:**
> Sarah hires a plumber to fix a leak in her house.

1.  **Offer & Acceptance:** The plumber offers to fix the leak for $200. Sarah accepts.
2.  **Consideration:** The plumber gives his labor (fixing the pipe), and Sarah gives her money ($200).
3.  **Intention:** This is a commercial transaction, so it's clear both intended it to be legally binding.

Because all three elements are present, they have formed a valid contract. If the plumber fixes the pipe and Sarah refuses to pay, she has committed a **Breach of Contract**. The plumber can sue her in a civil court for the $200.

## Key Takeaways

*   A contract is a legally enforceable promise.
*   It requires an Offer, an Acceptance, and Consideration (an exchange of value).
*   Verbal contracts are valid, but written ones are much easier to enforce.
*   Breaking a contract is known as a "breach."

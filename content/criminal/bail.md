## Introduction

You often hear on the news that someone accused of a crime was "granted bail." But what does that mean? 

A fundamental rule of criminal justice is: **"Innocent until proven guilty."** When a person is arrested, their trial has not even begun yet. Keeping an innocent person in jail for years while waiting for a trial is unjust. This is why the concept of bail exists.

## What is Bail?

**Bail** is the temporary release of an accused person awaiting trial. It is an agreement that the person will show up for all court hearings; usually, they must deposit a sum of money or property as a guarantee.

If the person flees (jumps bail), they lose the money and a warrant is immediately issued for their arrest.

## Types of Offenses Let's Talk Bail

1.  **Bailable Offenses:** Minor crimes (e.g., minor scuffles, mischief). In these cases, getting bail is a *right*. The police officer at the station can grant bail if you provide the required surety.
2.  **Non-Bailable Offenses:** Serious crimes (e.g., murder, kidnapping). Here, bail is *not a right*. Only a judge can grant bail at their discretion.

## Real-Life Example

> **Scenario:**
> John is accused of stealing a laptop. He is arrested and placed in police custody.

John's lawyer argues before the judge that John has a job, a family, and is not a "flight risk" (meaning he won't run away). The judge agrees and grants bail, setting it at $5,000. 

John pays the court $5,000 (usually via a bail bondsman or putting up property). John is released and goes home. 
Six months later, during his trial, John shows up to court every day. At the end of the trial, regardless of whether he is found guilty or not guilty, the $5,000 is returned to him because he fulfilled his promise to appear in court. 

## Key Takeaways

*   **Bail is temporary release**, not an acquittal (being found innocent).
*   It ensures the accused appears for their trial.
*   "Bail is the rule, jail is an exception" because of the presumption of innocence.
*   The court holds money/property as leverage to ensure the person returns.

---

*Did You Know?* An "Anticipatory Bail" is a pre-arrest bail. If you fear you are going to be arrested based on false accusations, you can apply for bail to a high court before the police even arrest you!

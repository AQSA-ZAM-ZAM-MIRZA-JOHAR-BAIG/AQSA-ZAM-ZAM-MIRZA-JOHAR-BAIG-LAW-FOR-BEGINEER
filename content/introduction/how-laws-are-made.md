## Introduction

Laws don't just appear out of nowhere. The process by which a rule becomes an official law of the land is called the **legislative process**. In a democracy, this ensures that laws are debated, scrutinized, and approved by the elected representatives of the people.

## The Basic Steps (The Journey of a Bill)

Before a law is officially passed, it is called a **Bill** (a proposed law).

### 1. Introduction of the Bill
A member of the legislative body (like Parliament or Congress) introduces the draft of the proposed law.

### 2. Discussion and Debate
The bill is read and discussed. Lawmakers debate its pros and cons. They might suggest amendments (changes).

### 3. Committee Review
Often, the bill is sent to a specialized committee (a smaller group of experts) for in-depth study. They might call experts to testify and further refine the bill.

### 4. Voting
The bill is brought back to the main legislative body for a final vote. It usually needs a majority (more than 50%) to pass.

### 5. Approval by the Other House (if applicable)
In countries with a bicameral legislature (two houses, like the Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha in India, or the House and Senate in the US), the bill must pass both houses.

### 6. Assent of the Head of State
Once the legislature approves the bill, it is sent to the Head of State (like the President). Once they sign it, the Bill becomes an **Act** (a Law).

## Real-Life Example

> **Scenario:**
> The government wants to ban a specific type of plastic.

The environment minister drafts a *ban on plastic bill*. It gets introduced in Parliament. MPs debate it—some arguing it hurts businesses, others saying it saves the environment. It goes to a committee, they modify it to allow a 6-month grace period. It passes both houses, the President signs it, and it officially becomes the *Plastic Waste Management Act*.

## Key Takeaways

*   A proposed law is a **Bill**. An enacted law is an **Act**.
*   Laws must be debated and voted upon by elected representatives.
*   The final step usually requires the signature of the head of state.

## Introduction

When people talk about "the law," they are usually referring to one of two main branches: **Civil Law** or **Criminal Law**. Knowing the difference is crucial because they handle entirely different types of problems and have different consequences.

## Criminal Law: Crimes Against Society

Criminal law deals with behavior that is considered an offense against society, the state, or the public, even if the immediate victim is an individual.

*   **Examples:** Murder, assault, theft, drunk driving.
*   **Who charges the crime?** The government (prosecutor/police) files the case, not the victim.
*   **Burden of Proof:** "Beyond a reasonable doubt" (very high standard).
*   **Punishment:** Fines, imprisonment, or even the death penalty.

## Civil Law: Disputes Between Individuals

Civil law deals with behavior that constitutes an injury to an individual or other private party, such as a corporation.

*   **Examples:** Defamation (including libel and slander), breach of contract, negligence resulting in injury or death, property damage.
*   **Who files the case?** The victim (the plaintiff) files a lawsuit against the party that caused the harm (the defendant).
*   **Burden of Proof:** "Preponderance of the evidence" (it is more likely than not that the defendant is responsible).
*   **Punishment:** Usually financial compensation (damages) or an order to do/not do something (injunction). Nobody goes to jail in a strictly civil case.

## Real-Life Example

> **Scenario:** 
> O.J. Simpson's trials are a classic example. 

He was first tried in a **criminal court** for murder. The burden of proof was "beyond a reasonable doubt," and he was found *not guilty*.
Later, the victims' families sued him in a **civil court** for wrongful death. The burden of proof was lower ("preponderance of the evidence"), and he was found *liable* and ordered to pay millions in damages.

## Key Takeaways

*   Criminal law = offenses against society; may result in jail time.
*   Civil law = disputes between individuals or entities; usually results in financial compensation.
*   The same act can lead to both criminal and civil cases.
